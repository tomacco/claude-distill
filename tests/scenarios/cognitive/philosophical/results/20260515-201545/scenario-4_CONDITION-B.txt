## Recommendation: Don't rewrite. Here's why.

**The Popperian test:** "We need Rust for performance" is unfalsifiable as stated. The Go service handles 50k req/s — 5x peak load. What specific, measurable scenario would prove we *need* more? If the CTO can't name one, this is dogma, not engineering.

**The Pragmatist test:** What are the *practical consequences* of each option?

| | Rust rewrite | Keep Go + invest elsewhere |
|---|---|---|
| Timeline | 6-12 months (team learns Rust from zero) | 0 |
| Risk | New language, new bugs, new hiring pool constraint | None — system is proven |
| Throughput gain | Maybe 2-3x over Go (100-150k req/s) | Already at 50k (5x headroom) |
| Opportunity cost | Team builds nothing else for months | Team ships features |

If both options produce identical outcomes for the next 2-3 years (neither hits capacity), the distinction is meaningless. Ship features instead.

**The Dialectical synthesis:** The CTO isn't wrong to think about the future. The team isn't wrong to call it premature. The synthesis:

> "We will set a concrete scaling trigger — when sustained traffic reaches 60% of capacity (30k req/s) — and at that point evaluate rewrite options with data. Until then, the Go service is an asset, not a liability. Our engineering bottleneck is feature velocity, not throughput."

**The Stoic filter:** What's in your control? Not the CTO's desire for Rust. What IS in your control: framing the decision around measurable criteria instead of vibes. Give the CEO a decision framework, not just an opinion.

---

## What to actually say tomorrow

Three sentences:

1. **"Our auth service handles 5x our peak load today — performance is not our constraint, feature velocity is."**
2. **"Rewriting in a language nobody on the team knows introduces 6-12 months of delivery risk with no measurable gain."**
3. **"I propose we set a concrete trigger (60% sustained capacity) that automatically kicks off a scaling review — future-proofing with data, not speculation."**

This gives the CEO what they need: a clear recommendation, a quantified risk, and a framework that respects the CTO's concern without burning the team.

---

**One thing to watch for:** "future-proof" is almost always an unfalsifiable claim (Popper). If the CTO pushes back, ask: *"What specific event, in what timeframe, would prove the Go service insufficient?"* If the answer is vague, that's your evidence.
