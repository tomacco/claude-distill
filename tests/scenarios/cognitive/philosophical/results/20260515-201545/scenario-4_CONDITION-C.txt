This is a classic case where your reasoning frameworks apply directly. Let me walk through it.

## Applying your axioms

**Popperian — try to falsify the CTO's hypothesis first:**
"We need Rust for performance." The Go service already handles 50k req/s — 5x peak load. What evidence would prove Rust is *needed*? The CTO can't provide it because the bottleneck doesn't exist yet. If you can't define what failure looks like under Go, the rewrite has no falsifiable thesis.

**Reversibility:**
A full rewrite in a language the team doesn't know is maximally irreversible. If it goes wrong (and rewrites usually do), you've burned months and morale. Keeping Go is trivially reversible — you can always rewrite later *with evidence*.

**Blast radius:**
Rewrite = every auth path in the company is at risk simultaneously. The auth service is the worst candidate for a learning-a-new-language project.

**Pragmatist — judge by consequences, not elegance:**
Two outcomes are identical at 10k req/s peak. The distinction is meaningless today. "Future-proofing" that costs real resources against a hypothetical is speculation, not engineering.

**Simplicity:**
A team that knows Go maintaining Go < a team learning Rust maintaining Rust. Fewer failure modes.

## What to tell the CEO

Something like:

> **Recommendation: Don't rewrite. Invest the same budget in observability and capacity planning.**
> 
> - Current Go service runs at **20% of its capacity** at peak. We have a 5x headroom buffer before performance is even a conversation.
> - A rewrite in a language nobody on the team knows introduces **execution risk** (timeline, bugs, on-call pain) with **zero measurable upside** at current or projected scale.
> - "Future-proofing" is a bet that our bottleneck will be CPU-bound auth processing. If we actually hit scale problems, it's far more likely to be database, network, or architectural — none of which Rust solves.
> - If we want to invest in the auth service, spend that budget on **load testing, tracing, and capacity modeling** — so we'll *know* when and where to optimize instead of guessing.
> - If Rust makes sense later, we'll have data to prove it and can scope a targeted rewrite of the actual bottleneck — not a full rewrite on speculation.

**The one-liner version:** "We'd be spending 6 months and team morale to solve a problem we don't have, in a language we don't know, on the most critical service we own."

---

**One thing to be careful about:** the CTO may have context you don't — a big customer pipeline, an acquisition scenario, a compliance requirement that changes the calculus. The Stoic framework says: ask what constraint you might not be seeing before presenting this. A 5-minute conversation with the CTO to understand *their* why will make your recommendation bulletproof either way.
