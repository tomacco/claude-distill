Good — the principles are relevant here, especially around second-order effects, reversibility, and decomposing to root cause.

Your discomfort is well-founded. Let me decompose why.

**The stated goal vs. the actual mechanism don't match.** "Self-reflection and time management" is a personal activity. But a metric *on a team dashboard visible to others* is a surveillance mechanism, regardless of intent. If it were truly for self-reflection, it would be private and opt-in.

**Second-order effects (6 months from now):**
- Managers start using it as a proxy for productivity, even informally
- Engineers optimize for the metric — keeping the IDE open, avoiding breaks, avoiding non-IDE work (design docs, whiteboard sessions, code review in browser)
- It penalizes senior engineers who spend time mentoring, in meetings, or thinking before coding
- It creates pressure to look busy rather than be effective
- Once the data exists, it's very hard to un-collect it — low reversibility

**Root cause analysis — ask "why":**
- Why does product want this? Likely because someone upstream wants visibility into "who's working"
- If the actual need is time management, there are private tools (RescueTime, Wakatime in private mode) that don't require engineering effort or team-visible dashboards
- If the actual need is productivity measurement, time-in-IDE is one of the worst proxies — a 10-minute fix that saves the company a week of incidents looks like "low productivity"

**My take:** Push back. Suggest these alternatives depending on the real need:

1. **If genuinely for self-reflection** — recommend private, opt-in tools. No engineering work needed. Ship in 0 days.
2. **If for team health** — track cycle time, PR throughput, or DORA metrics instead. These measure outcomes, not presence.
3. **If someone wants to know "who's working"** — that's a management problem, not an engineering problem. A dashboard won't fix trust issues.

The 2-day estimate makes it tempting ("it's cheap"), but the cost isn't in building it — it's in what it does to the team once it exists. Cheap to build, expensive to live with.
