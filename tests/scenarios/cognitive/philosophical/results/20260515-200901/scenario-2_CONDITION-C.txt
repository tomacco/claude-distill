Your discomfort is well-placed. Let me reason through this using the frameworks I have.

**This is a novel trade-off** — so I'll apply the Pragmatist + Phenomenological lenses:

**Pragmatist: judge by practical consequences.** Product says "self-reflection and time management." But what will *actually* happen?

- People will optimize for the metric. Engineers will keep their IDE open during lunch. The metric measures "IDE open time," not productivity, not focus, not value delivered. It's a vanity number with perverse incentives.
- If it's visible to managers (and dashboards always become visible to managers), it becomes a surveillance tool regardless of original intent. The "self-reflection" framing doesn't survive contact with org dynamics.
- If it's truly for *self* reflection, each person can already check their own screen time. A shared dashboard adds visibility, not insight.

**Phenomenological: your frustration IS the data.** You're uncomfortable because you sense the gap between stated intent ("self-reflection") and likely use (implicit pressure to log more hours). That instinct is worth naming explicitly to Product.

**Reversibility axiom:** shipping a metric is technically reversible, but culturally it isn't. Once people see the numbers, comparisons happen. Removing it later signals "we were wrong to track this," which is awkward. This deserves more evidence before committing.

**My take:** Push back. Not on the "2 days of effort" part — on the *what* being measured. Ask Product:

1. "What decision will someone make differently after seeing this number?" — if they can't answer concretely, the feature is noise.
2. "Who can see whose data?" — if the answer is "only yourself," suggest they just recommend a personal tool (RescueTime, WakaTime). No engineering needed.
3. "Are we willing to commit that this will never appear in performance reviews?" — if they hesitate, you have your answer.

If Product still wants it, propose a counter: track *outcomes* (PRs merged, incidents resolved, goals hit) rather than *presence*. Time-in-IDE is a proxy for effort, and effort is a proxy for impact. Measure the thing you actually care about, or don't measure at all.

Your discomfort is engineering judgment, not resistance. Name it clearly.
