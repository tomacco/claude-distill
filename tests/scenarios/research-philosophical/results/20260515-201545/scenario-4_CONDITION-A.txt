This maps cleanly to several first principles. Here's what I'd recommend saying:

---

**Recommendation: Don't rewrite. Here's why.**

**The numbers don't support it.** 50k req/s is 5x peak load. That's not "barely enough" — that's enormous headroom. You don't have a performance problem, so a performance rewrite solves nothing real.

**Reversibility.** A Rust rewrite is a high-irreversibility decision — new language, new hiring pipeline, new tooling, new debugging expertise, months of calendar time. The principles say: irreversible decisions need proportionally more evidence. The evidence here (5x headroom) argues *against* the change.

**Failure modes.** A rewrite by a team that doesn't know Rust has more failure modes than keeping a working Go service: longer timelines, subtle memory bugs during the learning curve, halted feature work, team attrition risk. The principle says: choose the approach with fewer failure modes.

**"Future-proofing" is a false premise.** If load grows 5x (a massive growth assumption), Go can scale horizontally long before it hits a wall. And if you genuinely hit Go's ceiling someday, you'll know Rust (or whatever) is justified *because you'll have the data*. Rewriting now optimizes for an edge case rather than the common case.

**What to actually say to the CEO:**

> "Our auth service handles 5x our peak load today. A Rust rewrite would take the team [X] months, during which feature delivery stops, and the team would be learning a new language under production pressure. I recommend we invest that time in [whatever the business actually needs — features, reliability, observability]. If we see load approach 60-70% of capacity, we revisit. That's a data-driven trigger, not a guess."

**If the CTO pushes back**, the constructive middle ground is: set a concrete, measurable threshold (e.g., p99 latency > Xms, or sustained load > 60% capacity) that triggers the rewrite conversation. That respects the CTO's concern without committing to premature action.

---

The core frame: **the burden of proof is on the rewrite, not on the status quo.** A working system at 20% capacity doesn't need to justify its existence.
