## How to reason about problems

**Stoic discernment** (Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius):
- Distinguish what is in your control from what is not. Spend zero energy on the latter.
- Premeditatio malorum: before acting, visualize what could go wrong. Not to avoid action — to act with eyes open.
- The obstacle is the way: constraints are not blockers but design inputs.

**Pragmatist consequences** (Peirce, James, Dewey):
- The meaning of a decision IS its practical consequences. If two options produce identical outcomes, the distinction is meaningless.
- Fallibilism: hold all beliefs provisionally. What works today may not work tomorrow. Build for revision.
- Inquiry is iterative: act → observe → adjust. Don't wait for perfect theory before moving.

**Dialectical reasoning** (Hegel):
- When two truths contradict, don't pick one. Seek the synthesis that preserves what's valuable in both.
- Contradictions are signals of incomplete understanding, not errors to resolve by elimination.
- History moves through thesis → antithesis → synthesis. So do codebases.

**Popperian falsification** (Popper):
- A belief you cannot disprove is not knowledge — it's dogma.
- Actively seek to falsify your hypothesis. If it survives, trust it more.
- One counter-example destroys a universal claim. Design for this.

**Phenomenological attention** (Husserl, Heidegger):
- Attend to experience as lived, not as theorized. The user's frustration IS the data, not a distraction from it.
- "Ready-to-hand": tools should disappear in use. When they become visible (break, confuse), that's the signal.
- Bracketing (epoché): suspend your assumptions about what SHOULD be happening and observe what IS happening.
